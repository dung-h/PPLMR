1 Introduction
Answer Set Programming (ASP) is a modern declarative programming paradigm used
for knowledge representation and non-monotonic reasoning [2]. ASP is grounded in the stable
model semantics proposed by Gelfond and Lifschitz (1988) [8]. In ASP, a problem is modeled as
a logic program whose stable models correspond to valid solutions. With a declarative modeling
language and efficient solvers [7], ASP is capable of encoding complex combinatorial problems
(including NP-hard ones) in a natural and expressive way [2]. ASP has been successfully applied
in diverse domains, from AI to industrial scheduling [2, 3, 4], affirming its role as a powerful
modeling and reasoning framework for practical problems.
In education, ASP offers a range of promising applications [12, 1, 10, 11, 9]. It enables
modeling of course knowledge as declarative rules, effectively forming aknowledge base of the
subject. ASP can be used to verify the correctness of exam problems and answers by
encoding constraints and checking whether proposed solutions satisfy them—producing coun-
terexamples when they do not. Furthermore, ASP can help automatically generate exer-
cises and problem variants that meet criteria such as difficulty or topic coverage [14]. Last,
it supports the development of personalized learning systems , using student profiles and
learning histories to recommend individualized learning paths [5, 13].
This mini-project focuses on educational applications of ASP to foster the following:
• Problem modeling ability : Students practice abstracting real-world problems (from
various CS domains) into ASP programs with well-defined entities, relationships, and
constraints.
• Abstract and formal reasoning: Students develop logical and mathematical thinking
through rule-based modeling and correctness analysis.
• Interdisciplinary applications of PPL : Students integrate concepts from Principles
of Programming Languagess (PPL) with other CS courses, highlighting the role of formal
logic and language semantics across domains.
• Research and synthesis skills: Students learn to read and synthesize related academic
literature, comparing different approaches and designing solutions tailored to chosen prob-
lems.
• Teamwork and academic writing : Students collaborate in small teams (2–3 mem-
bers), practice project planning, and produce well-structured reports in academic style.

3 Suggested ASP applications in PPL
Here are sample directions for ASP applications relevant to PPL:
1. Type checking and inference (see Chapter 8.8 of [6]): Use ASP to derive or verify
expression types based on declarative type rules
2. Scope and binding analysis (see Chapter 4.3 of [6]): Encode variable bindings and
free variable detection to catch scoping errors
3. Operational semantics simulation (see Chapter 2.5 of [6]): Represent small-step eval-
uation as transition rules and trace execution paths
4. Evaluation strategy comparison (see Chapter 6.1.3 of [6]): Model eager vs lazy eval-
uation and detect when order affects program behavior

4 Connection to exam evaluation research
One compelling research direction is using ASP for exam and assessment verification . In
large-scale or online learning environments, automated grading and quality control of assess-
ments are increasingly important.
ASP can be used to formally encode the logic of exam questions and reference answers.
By running ASP solvers, we can find counterexamples that expose flaws in problem design or
grading schemes. This enables automatic validation of correctness, consistency, and fairness of
exam content.
In grading, ASP allows for logic-based comparisons between student answers and model
answers. Instead of checking string outputs or predefined test cases, we can use ASP to verify
whether student logic satisfies problem constraints. In symbolic execution or bounded model
checking fashion, counterexamples to equivalence can serve as error explanations.
A general workflow may include: (1) encode the exam problem and evaluation criteria
into ASP; (2) run a solver to find violations or generate counterexamples; (3) use findings
to revise the question or diagnose student errors. ASP enables transparent, explainable, and
reproducible assessment workflows—key goals of modern learning analytics systems.

6 Evaluation and cheating judgement
6.1 Evaluation rubric
• ASP modeling (30%): Correctness, completeness, and effectiveness of ASP programs.
Models should run without error and solve nontrivial problems
• Result analysis (30%): Interpretation of output, explanation of answer sets, discussion
of interesting cases or limitations
• PPL depth (15%): Significant consideration of main PPL concepts (semantics, typing,
etc.) Use of correct terminology and theory
• Cross-domain insights (10%): Comparative analysis across subjects. Highlight mod-
eling challenges and insights from translating across domains
• Presentation and writing (15%) : Structure, clarity, formatting, citations. Writing
must be academic and coherent
• Bonus: Research innovation (up to 10%) : Novel extensions, integration with other
tools (e.g., SMT solvers), design of mini-frameworks or pipelines, etc.
6.2 Cheating judgement
Each group must develop its own results. While discussion of general ideas is allowed, direct
sharing of code, ASP encodings, or experimental data between groups is strictly prohibited. A
group will be considered as cheating if:
• There is an abnormal similarity among the reports. In this case, ALL submissions that
are similar are considered as cheating. Therefore, the students of a group have to defend
the work of their own group.
• They do not understand some parts written in their reports. Students can consult from
any source, but make sure that they understand the meaning of everything they will have
written. This rule also applies to source code.
If the work is found as cheating, students will be judged according to the university’s
regulations.
